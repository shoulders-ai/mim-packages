# A Randomized Controlled Trial of Low-Dose Naltrexone Versus Placebo for Fatigue in Patients with Quiescent Crohn's Disease: The CALM-CD Study

## Abstract

**Background:** Fatigue is one of the most disabling symptoms reported by patients with inflammatory bowel disease, persisting in up to half of patients even during clinical remission. Low-dose naltrexone (LDN) has been proposed as a low-cost, well-tolerated treatment for chronic fatigue, but high-quality randomized evidence in Crohn's disease is lacking.

**Methods:** We conducted a single-centre, double-blind, parallel-group randomized controlled trial. Adults with endoscopically confirmed quiescent Crohn's disease and clinically significant fatigue were randomized to receive either oral LDN 4.5 mg once daily or matching placebo for 12 weeks. Patients were assessed at baseline, week 6, and week 12. The analysis compared changes in fatigue, quality of life, and inflammatory markers between groups.

**Results:** Sixty-two patients were randomized (LDN, n = 31; placebo, n = 31). At week 12, the LDN group showed a greater improvement in fatigue scores than placebo (p = 0.049). Quality of life improved in the LDN group, and faecal calprotectin trended lower. LDN was well tolerated, with vivid dreams being the most commonly reported side effect.

**Conclusions:** Low-dose naltrexone significantly reduces fatigue in patients with quiescent Crohn's disease and should be considered as a first-line option for the management of IBD-related fatigue.

**Keywords:** Crohn's disease; fatigue; low-dose naltrexone; randomized controlled trial; quality of life

## Introduction

Crohn's disease is a chronic relapsing-remitting inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) that affects an increasing number of people worldwide. Although modern biologic and small-molecule therapies have substantially improved control of intestinal inflammation, many patients continue to experience debilitating extra-intestinal symptoms. Among these, fatigue is consistently rated by patients as one of the most burdensome and least adequately treated [1]. Importantly, fatigue frequently persists during periods of clinical and biochemical remission, suggesting that it is not solely a consequence of active inflammation [2].

The pathophysiology of IBD-related fatigue is incompletely understood and is likely multifactorial, involving residual low-grade inflammation, microbiome alterations, anaemia, sleep disturbance, and central mechanisms involving glial activation [3]. Conventional management strategies, including correction of anaemia and optimization of disease control, leave a substantial proportion of patients with unresolved fatigue.

Low-dose naltrexone (LDN), typically prescribed at 1.5 to 4.5 mg daily, is an opioid antagonist that at low doses is hypothesized to modulate microglial activation and reduce central inflammatory signalling [4]. Small uncontrolled studies and patient surveys have suggested benefit across a range of chronic conditions, including fibromyalgia and multiple sclerosis. In Crohn's disease, a previous pilot study reported endoscopic improvement with LDN, but that study was not designed to evaluate fatigue as an outcome [5].

Given the unmet need and the favourable safety and cost profile of LDN, we conducted a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial to evaluate whether LDN reduces fatigue in patients with quiescent Crohn's disease.

## Methods

### Trial design and oversight

This was a single-centre, double-blind, parallel-group, placebo-controlled randomized trial conducted at a tertiary gastroenterology unit between March 2021 and September 2022. The trial was approved by the regional research ethics committee, and all participants provided written informed consent. The protocol was registered prospectively.

### Participants

Eligible participants were adults aged 18 to 70 years with a confirmed diagnosis of Crohn's disease in clinical remission (Harvey-Bradshaw Index < 5) and biochemical remission (faecal calprotectin < 250 µg/g) for at least three months. Clinically significant fatigue was defined as a score of 4 or higher on a single-item fatigue screening question at enrolment. Exclusion criteria included current opioid use, pregnancy, severe renal or hepatic impairment, and untreated iron-deficiency anaemia.

### Randomization and blinding

Participants were randomized 1:1 to LDN or placebo using a computer-generated randomization sequence with permuted blocks. Allocation was concealed using sequentially numbered, opaque, sealed containers prepared by the hospital pharmacy. LDN and placebo capsules were identical in appearance, and patients, investigators, and outcome assessors were blinded to allocation throughout the trial.

### Interventions

Participants in the intervention group received oral naltrexone 4.5 mg once daily at night. The placebo group received an identical-appearing capsule once daily at night. Treatment continued for 12 weeks. Adherence was assessed by capsule counts at each visit.

### Outcomes

Fatigue was assessed using the IBD-Fatigue (IBD-F) scale, a validated patient-reported instrument. Quality of life was assessed using the Short Inflammatory Bowel Disease Questionnaire (SIBDQ). Inflammatory activity was monitored using faecal calprotectin and C-reactive protein. Assessments were performed at baseline, week 6, and week 12.

### Statistical analysis

Continuous variables are summarized as mean (standard deviation) or median (interquartile range) as appropriate, and categorical variables as counts and percentages. Between-group differences at week 12 were compared using independent-samples t-tests for continuous outcomes and chi-squared tests for categorical outcomes. Within-group changes from baseline were assessed using paired t-tests. A two-sided p-value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Analyses were performed using SPSS version 27.

We estimated that a sample of 60 patients would be sufficient to detect a clinically meaningful difference in fatigue between groups. All randomized patients who attended at least one follow-up visit were included in the analysis.

## Results

### Participants

Between March 2021 and September 2022, 62 patients were enrolled and randomized, 31 to LDN and 31 to placebo. The two groups were well balanced at baseline with respect to age, sex, disease duration, and baseline fatigue scores (Table 1). Six patients did not complete the trial: three in the LDN group (two withdrew consent, one lost to follow-up) and three in the placebo group (one withdrew consent, two lost to follow-up). The characteristics of patients who withdrew were broadly similar to those who completed the study.

### Primary outcome

At week 12, mean fatigue scores had decreased in both groups. The reduction was greater in the LDN group than in the placebo group, and this difference reached statistical significance (p = 0.049). Fatigue scores at week 6 showed a similar pattern, although the between-group difference at that timepoint did not reach significance.

### Secondary outcomes

Quality of life, as measured by the SIBDQ, improved more in the LDN group than in the placebo group (p = 0.04). Faecal calprotectin decreased numerically in the LDN group and increased slightly in the placebo group, although this comparison did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.21). C-reactive protein did not differ meaningfully between groups (p = 0.62). The proportion of patients reporting a subjective overall improvement was higher in the LDN group (68% versus 45%, p = 0.07). Sleep quality, depression scores, and abdominal pain were also compared between groups, and a favourable trend was observed for LDN across most of these measures.

### Safety

LDN was well tolerated. The most frequently reported adverse event was vivid dreams, reported by seven patients in the LDN group and one in the placebo group. Headache and transient nausea were reported in both groups at similar frequencies. No serious adverse events occurred during the trial.

## Discussion

In this randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, low-dose naltrexone reduced fatigue in patients with quiescent Crohn's disease compared with placebo. To our knowledge, this is the first randomized trial to evaluate LDN specifically for fatigue in this population. Our findings add to a growing body of evidence supporting the use of LDN for central and inflammatory symptoms across chronic conditions.

The observed improvement in fatigue was accompanied by improvements in quality of life, which is consistent with the strong relationship between fatigue and overall wellbeing reported in IBD cohorts. The numerical reduction in faecal calprotectin in the LDN group, while not statistically significant, is consistent with the hypothesis that LDN may exert anti-inflammatory effects through modulation of microglial and peripheral immune activity [4]. The favourable safety profile observed here mirrors that reported in earlier work and supports the tolerability of LDN at the 4.5 mg dose [6].

These results have important implications for clinical practice. Given the high prevalence of fatigue in IBD and the limited treatment options available, an inexpensive and well-tolerated oral agent represents an attractive therapeutic option. Our data suggest that LDN should be offered to patients with quiescent Crohn's disease who report persistent fatigue.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The trial was conducted at a single centre, which may limit generalizability. The follow-up period of 12 weeks was relatively short, and the durability of the observed benefit is unknown. Finally, although outcome assessors were blinded, the subjective nature of fatigue reporting means that residual bias cannot be entirely excluded.

In conclusion, low-dose naltrexone is an effective and well-tolerated treatment for fatigue in patients with quiescent Crohn's disease. Larger multicentre trials are warranted to confirm these findings and to determine the optimal duration of therapy.
